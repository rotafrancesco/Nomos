Editor's Note

First Dispatch: Why Nomos

An introduction to this journal, and to the concept it borrows its name from.

Francesco — Editor

Every order of things rests on a prior act of division: a line drawn across land or law that decides who counts, and by what right. The Greeks called that founding act nomos — not merely statute, but the deeper settlement from which statute becomes possible. This journal takes its name from that idea, and from the more particular use Carl Schmitt made of it: the claim that international order is not a neutral given, but a historically specific arrangement, drawn up by particular powers, and open to contest by those it excludes.

That contest is the subject matter here. Dispatches and essays on the Horn of Africa, on the Middle East, and on the actors, state and non-state alike, who test the limits of who gets to claim legitimate authority. The approach is analytical rather than advocative: named sources, grounded political economy, and an even hand toward positions the writer may not share.

On the Name

Nomos (νόμος): in Greek thought, law or convention as against nature; in Carl Schmitt's usage, the founding division of the earth from which legal and political order follows. This journal borrows the word to mark its subject: not just events, but the settlements beneath them.
