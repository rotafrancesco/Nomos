When Peace Costs Too Much

On the manufactured stability of Congo's forever war

Francesco — Contributor

The phone in your hand, the screen you touch, the battery that powers it: all of it is tied to one of the most brutal and persistent wars of our time. For over two decades the Democratic Republic of Congo has lived inside violence, and no end appears near. North Kivu, South Kivu, and Ituri are the regions hit hardest: zones of mass displacement where militias command and the state barely exists.

Faced with a crisis this vast and this prolonged, the common explanation reaches for "African instability", state failure, ethnic rivalry. It is a narrative that locates the problem inside Congo's own borders and turns it into an internal failure. But this reading "fails to capture the integrated, economic, and transnational nature of the conflict" (HCSS 2013).

The reality is more complex, and more uncomfortable for us: instability here is not a failure, it is a business model. A model that pays local, regional, and global actors alike, and that runs on our own near-total dependence on technology. At its centre sit two minerals, coltan and cobalt, both indispensable to the global technology economy, present in nearly every electronic component that exists. We demand these materials, and by extension, we demand the violence that comes with them.

Violence as a Business Model

Coltan and cobalt are not merely resources: they are the very structure of the conflict. In the mines of eastern Kivu, violence functions as a mode of territorial administration. Militias control villages, roads, rivers, and bridges; they tax trade; they impose tolls; they exploit forced labour; they punish those who "disobey". As Ojewale reconstructs it, "mineral extraction in Kivu takes place within an ecosystem where violence is the principal form of governance" (Ojewale 2022). The more the state disappears, the more profitable the mining economy becomes. The longer the war drags on, the more local actors learn to run it like an enterprise. Halting extraction would become too costly, and restoring order would cut into profits, since order brings oversight and taxation. The most convenient solution, then, appears to be only one: keep the instability controlled.

The UN Group of Experts has documented exactly this logic, reporting how the AFC/M23, backed by the Rwanda Defence Forces, systematically occupied strategic mining areas between 2023 and 2025 (UN GoE 2024; UN GoE 2025). These advances do not follow conventional military logic; they follow the map of the deposits at Rubaya, Masisi, and Rutshuru. Everything revolves around the economics of these materials. When Rubaya, the region's largest coltan deposit, fell under M23 control in 2024, it was not a simple episode of war: it was a change of economic management.

The Regional Rent: Rwanda, Uganda, and the M23

The figures tell their own story: at least 150 tonnes of coltan were illegally exported to Rwanda in 2024 alone, often blended with domestic production to make them untraceable (UN GoE 2024). If Congo is the mineral heart, Rwanda and Uganda are the arteries through which the ore flows. This is where the second dimension of the conflict sits: regional political economy. UN reports have been clear for over a decade that Rwanda exports coltan in volumes that exceed its own productive capacity, because much of the mineral originates in Congo, crosses through the border posts of North Kivu, is mixed with domestic output, and emerges "laundered" onto international markets (UN GoE 2024). None of this is a secret; it happens in plain sight, because it is intrinsically accepted by the commercial model. This rent sustains Rwanda's image as an "African development success", funds infrastructure programmes, and reinforces the political capital of the Kagame government. The M23 is the operational arm of this model. Its offensives do not chase ideological goals; they chase mines, trade routes, and logistical hubs. For some analysts, it is not a militia at all: it is a military infrastructure for the mineral trade. Uganda follows a similar logic, particularly in Ituri and North Kivu, where it maintains commercial and political networks dating back to the regional wars of 1998 to 2003.

For both countries, peace in Congo would be counterproductive: controlled borders, transparent mining contracts, taxes paid to the Congolese state, less room for opaque intermediation. Stability in Congo would mean the end of a rent. So it is avoided.

Where the Trail Goes Cold

And yet the real difference plays out thousands of kilometres away. Without global demand, this entire system would collapse in a day. Coltan extracted in Kivu is sold to middlemen, transported into Rwanda or Uganda, blended with minerals from legal mines, and shipped to smelters in China, Malaysia, or South Korea. Once refined, it loses all identity: it is no longer "conflict coltan", it is simply an input for the electronics industry. This is where traceability mechanisms are meant to intervene.

According to the OECD, many supply chains from high-risk zones "remain exposed to unverifiable intermediation practices" (OECD 2016). EU Regulation 2017/821, while symbolically important, only reaches direct importers. It is like placing a filter at the mouth of a river rather than at its source. The situation closely resembles what The Sentry documented for the gold trade, describing "a global system that allows conflict minerals to reach European and American markets bearing certificates of good conduct" (The Sentry 2018).

The heart of the problem is this: the supply chain works because it is designed not to see.

The West's Responsibility by Omission

There is a fourth dimension, usually underweighted: the responsibility of Western governments. It is a responsibility of omission rather than action. Deliberate choices are made not to intervene, and this leaves the field open to everyone who profits from the arrangement. The regulatory system the West has put in place is itself largely symbolic: the OECD guidelines are voluntary, EU Regulation 2017/821 is narrow in scope, and in the United States, Section 1502 of the Dodd-Frank Act has been hollowed out, turning due diligence into a symbolic exercise. This machinery of non-action lets companies keep importing minerals through intermediaries without real consequences.

Geopolitically, Western responsibility runs just as deep. Rwanda and Uganda are crucial Western allies in peacekeeping missions, counter-insurgency operations, and regional security programmes. Accusing Kigali or Kampala would carry a cost that few governments have the will, or the courage, to pay. Diplomatic silence is not a distraction: it is a strategic choice.

Conclusion: A Tragedy by Design

At this point, the picture is complete. Congo is not devastated by a conflict that has "spun out of control": it is devastated by a conflict kept under control precisely so that it remains useful, economically and politically.

Everyone would lose if peace arrived. Militias would lose their principal source of income; Rwanda and Uganda would lose mineral rents and regional influence; large technology firms would have to pay more for the same raw materials; Western states would see their energy transition slow down. Everyone would lose. Except the Congolese.

Congo is not an ignored tragedy. It is an accepted one. Its war does not continue because it is uncontrollable, but because it is convenient. And for as long as this equilibrium holds, the world will keep touching the screens of its phones without seeing the hands that, thousands of kilometres away, extract the mineral that makes them work.
