Growing up in the heart of Western modernity, we were socialised to interpret the world through a sharp, non-negotiable moral distinction between good and evil. This was not presented to us as a choice, but as a lens, the only lens through which a "civilised" subject could view reality. However, as Friedrich Nietzsche famously exposed in his *Genealogy of Morals*, this framework is not a reflection of universal truth, but a historical construct weaponised for dominance.

While this moral structure is deeply rooted in Christian theology, its genealogy precedes the Cross. As the philosopher Umberto Galimberti reminds us, Plato had already articulated the ideals of the Good, the True, and the Beautiful as the ultimate teleological aims of human life. This Platonic-Christian synthesis continues to shape the cognitive architecture of Western thought. It establishes a linear, often eschatological view of history where "our" values represent the terminus of moral progress. In this secularised religious narrative, the West is the "End of History," the final judge of what constitutes human dignity.

Within this cultural framework, Western societies tend to understand themselves through a specific, self-congratulatory moral vocabulary. We operate on the assumption that "values" are the fundamental, indivisible units distinguishing cultures. Differences are therefore interpreted as deep moral divides, suggesting that each culture is guided by an incompatible ethical core. From this perspective, geopolitical conflict appears not as the outcome of material friction, resource scarcity, or political strategy, but as the natural and tragic result of irreconcilable moral worlds.

This is a profound epistemological fallacy. Good and evil are not absolute, static monoliths; they are contingent upon perspective, context, and the locus of judgment. Our moral evaluations concern actions, and actions are always fallible, situated, and embedded in specific material conditions. To judge a culture by an abstract "value" is to ignore the structural machinery that makes life possible within it.

The conviction that cultures differ because they are guided by fundamentally different values is deeply misleading. It creates a "moral apartheid" that prevents us from seeing the shared structural foundations of human life. What differs is not the humanity of those who belong to other cultures, nor the basic concerns that orient their existence. The teleology of human life, the desire for flourishing, for continuity, for safety, remains an anthropological constant.

Care, responsibility, fear, and hope are not "Western" values; they are human imperatives. Violence and injustice are human possibilities distributed across all societies; they do not define the moral essence of a specific culture. When we say "Muslims value honour while we value freedom," we are engaging in a lazy form of cultural exoticism. Both cultures value both concepts; they simply organise them through different social technologies.

The true locus of difference lies elsewhere. It is not a matter of abstract "values," but of social ontology: how social relations are organised, how responsibility is distributed, and how authority is legitimised. What the public discourse lazily describes as a "clash of values" is, in reality, a friction between distinct social structures and historical experiences.

To understand this, we must abandon cultural psychology, which treats culture as a set of internal beliefs, and turn to structural sociology. Ann Swidler (1986), in her seminal work *Culture in Action*, dismantled the Weberian idea that culture is a unified system of values that pushes action in a consistent direction like a locomotive. Swidler argues that culture is not a psychological distinctiveness of values but a "tool kit" or "repertoire" of habits, skills, and styles from which people construct "strategies of action."

In "settled" cultural periods, people may profess high ideals (like "freedom" or "democracy"), but their actual actions are dictated by the structural realities of their "tool kit," the habits they have mastered to survive. The error of the West lies in assuming that Muslims act in a certain way because of "different values." Swidler demonstrates that this is a category error: people do not act based on abstract values, but based on the cultural equipment available to solve the problems of collective life.

The "clash" is not between those who love freedom and those who love oppression; it is a collision between two different technologies of social organisation. If a woman in a traditional society prioritises family over individual career, it is not necessarily because she "values" freedom less, but because her "tool kit" for survival and flourishing is built on relational reliability rather than atomised market competition.

### Technologies of the Self: Autonomy vs. Relational Discipline

When we look at Islamic societies, the Western gaze often falls into a simple binary: 'Secular Freedom' versus 'Religious Submission.' We assume that if you aren't fighting the system, you are a victim of it. This is the liberal obsession with "resistance" as the only form of valid agency. But a closer look reveals that Islamic ethics are chasing the same goals (justice, responsibility, and care) just through a completely different structural machine.

The anthropologist Saba Mahmood exploded this lazy thinking in her study of the piety movement in Egypt, *Politics of Piety*. She challenged the liberal assumption that agency is synonymous with the subversion of norms. In the West, we only recognise you as a free agent if you are rebelling against tradition. Mahmood argues, however, that in the Islamic tradition, agency isn't about rebellion; it's about docility.

We must be precise here. Mahmood is not using 'docility' in the modern sense of mindless subservience or being a doormat. She retrieves the term in its classical sense (from the Latin *docilitas*): the capacity to be taught. It implies a deliberate malleability, an active willingness to be shaped, instructed, and honed by a tradition.

Think of an apprentice learning a complex craft. He does not submit to his master out of weakness; he submits out of ambition. He makes himself 'docile' (teachable) because he understands that the only way to acquire the skill is to let the discipline transform him. In this view, submission is not an abandonment of the self; it is the rigorous method of constructing a higher self.

Think of a concert pianist. To become a master, she cannot just play whatever she feels like; she must submit to the rigorous, repetitive discipline of scales. She gives up the 'freedom' to bang on the keys randomly to acquire the capacity to create art. Mahmood argues the pious Muslim subject is doing the same thing. She submits to rituals, prayer, conduct, and the veil, not because she has no will, but to organise her will toward a specific goal: moral perfection.

Here is where the Western mind hits a wall. We look at the veil and ask: 'But is she free to take it off?' If the answer is no, we call it oppression. We define freedom as Choice, the ability to say no. But the Islamic tradition defines freedom as Capacity, the ability to inhabit the Truth.

For the pious woman Mahmood studied, the freedom to 'not wear it' is like the freedom of the pianist to not practise: it is a slavery to incompetence. The veil is not a shackle, but a tool, a 'technology of the self' used to build a specific kind of soul. Here lies the fundamental gap: the West believes the self is something you express by breaking rules. The Islamic tradition believes the self is something you construct by following them. To the liberal mind, she has lost her freedom; to the ethical mind, she has built her character. This is not a difference in values; it is a difference in the technology of personhood.

### The Pathology of Power: The State as Usurper

What we are seeing today in Iran, Afghanistan, or elsewhere is not "Islam in action", but power using religion as a uniform. The key mistake of Western commentary is to treat authoritarian violence as a cultural expression, rather than as a political strategy. These regimes do not enforce ethics; they enforce obedience. They have hijacked the "tool kit" of tradition to serve the "tool kit" of the security state.

Take Iran's Morality Police. In Islamic ethics, modesty (*haya*) is meaningful only if it is an internal state, a chosen discipline of the soul. It is supposed to shape the inner self, not merely regulate outward appearance. Once the state sends armed officers to patrol women's bodies, modesty stops being a moral practice and becomes a test of submission to the regime.

The hijab turns from an ethical tool into a political checkpoint. The aim is not virtue, but control: to make dissent visible, punishable, and public. Fear replaces conscience. Surveillance replaces responsibility. This is a betrayal of the very tradition the regime claims to protect. By making the veil compulsory through state violence, the regime effectively destroys its moral value, turning an act of piety into an act of coerced conformity.

The Taliban operate in much the same way. Their claim to Islamic legitimacy relies on public displays of punishment: executions, beatings, bans enforced at gunpoint. Yet historically, Islamic law (Sharia) treated punishment as a last resort, surrounded by such high evidentiary thresholds precisely to avoid injustice. The ethical logic of the tradition was restraint, not spectacle.

Authoritarian regimes reverse this logic. They make punishment central because it is efficient. Violence becomes a performance of power, not a search for justice. What is being defended is not moral order, but the authority of the regime. This pattern reveals what is really happening: religious language is being emptied of its ethical content and repurposed as state propaganda. Rituals remain, but their meaning is stripped away. What survives is a simplified, hardened version of tradition that can be enforced by police, prisons, and informants. This is not continuity with the past, but a modern invention: the fusion of religion with the surveillance state.

The same distortion appears in how these regimes deal with dissent. When protesters are shot in the streets or tortured in custody, the state does exactly what any corrupt dictatorship does: it lies, it covers up, it protects its own. There is no "witnessing against oneself," no accountability, no justice beyond loyalty to the regime. By the standards of their own religious texts, these governments are not guardians of justice, but its negation. They invoke God to shield institutions of violence. Seen this way, the problem is not Islam, but power. These regimes do not rule in the name of religion; they rule over it.

### The Great Western Misreading: Validating Oppression through "Culture"

The most insidious aspect of this phenomenon is how Western political discourse consumes these images of oppression. When the West sees the Morality Police or the Taliban, it does not see a political pathology; it sees "Islam". By interpreting state violence as a cultural artifact, the West reinforces the very binary these regimes desire.

Ironically, the Western liberal and the Eastern autocrat often agree on one point: that this violence is the "true" expression of Islamic values. The autocrat uses this to claim divine legitimacy; the Westerner uses it to justify the assumption of Islam as a fundamentally "Other" culture, built on values opposite to the West.

This is a strategic convenience for Western geopolitics. If the conflict in the Middle East is a "clash of values," then Western intervention or isolationism is morally justified as a defence of "civilisation" against "barbarism." By ignoring the material frictions and the modern, technological nature of these security states, Western observers treat a political struggle for power as an immutable religious essence. They mistake the uniform for the soul.

When we assume that the oppression in these states is "cultural," we effectively erase the millions of Muslims who resist these regimes precisely in the name of their own faith. By labelling the Taliban's bans as "Islamic values," we tell the Muslim activists fighting for education and justice that they are "un-Islamic." The West, in its lazy cultural psychology, validates the autocrat's monopoly on the faith. We accept the regime's definition of religion because it fits our pre-existing eschatological view of Western moral superiority.

### Democracy: Public Reason vs. Electoral Fetishism

Finally, we must deconstruct the idea that democracy is the exclusive property of the West. Amartya Sen (1999), in *Democracy as a Universal Value*, attacks this "cultural arrogance" head-on. Dictators across the global south favour the "Lee Kuan Yew" thesis, which argues that non-Western cultures prioritise order and discipline over freedom. Sen exposes this thesis as a self-interested lie propagated by elites to maintain control.

He contrasts the Western procedural definition of democracy (balloting and elections) with the substantive definition of democracy as Public Reasoning. Democracy is not just the act of voting; it is the capacity for public deliberation, criticism, and government accountability.

Sen cites the example of the Mughal Emperor Akbar in the sixteenth century. While Europe was burning heretics and drowning "witches," Akbar institutionalised religious tolerance and invited scholars of all faiths, Muslims, Hindus, Christians, Jains, to engage in public debate. He understood that the legitimacy of a ruler depends on his capacity to engage with the reason of his subjects.

The history of democracy is the history of public debate. Sen notes that no substantial famine has ever occurred in a functioning democracy, because the structure of public criticism forces the government to act. This mechanism of feedback and responsibility is perfectly consistent with the Islamic concept of Hisba, the duty to command right and forbid wrong, and Shura (consultation).

The current conflict arises not because Islam is incompatible with democracy, but because current political structures (often supported by the West for geopolitical convenience) deny Muslim societies the space for consultation and public debate that their own history shows was possible. When the West supports a dictator in the name of "stability," it is the West that is betraying democratic values, not the local culture.

### Cultural Imperialism and the Demand for "Modernity"

The problem is not cultural incompatibility, but the political and social conditions under which cultural differences are instrumentalised as irreconcilable. Talal Asad (1986) warns us against viewing Islam as a static culture, but rather as a "discursive tradition," a living argument linking the past to the future.

A tradition is not a museum; it is a conversation. When the West demands that Islam "modernise," it is usually asking Muslims to cease having that conversation with their own history and instead adopt the Western "tool kit" of individualism and secularism. This is an act of intellectual imperialism. It assumes that there is only one way to be modern, one way to be free, and one way to be rational.

Asad demonstrates that "Secularism" is not just the absence of religion, but a new way of organising power and bodies. It is not necessarily more "neutral" or "peaceful" than religion; it simply uses different justifications for its violence. By ignoring the complexity of Islamic discursive traditions, the West forces Muslims into a corner: either you become like us, or you are "medieval." This binary erases the possibility of an indigenous, Islamic path to accountability and justice.

### Conclusion: Ta'aruf as Radical Epistemology

Seen from this perspective, the idea of an inevitable "clash of civilisations" collapses. Differences between cultures do not negate shared humanity, nor do they preclude coexistence. On the contrary, they reveal multiple ways of articulating the same human concerns: how to live together, how to be just, how to protect the weak.

The core of the issue is the strategic misinterpretation of power. The West looks at the systems of power used by religious elites to oppress populations, systems that are political, modern, and structural, and interprets them as "Islamic values." This assumption allows the West to view Islam as a totally different and opposite culture, a move that serves to insulate Western identity and justify geopolitical actions. We take the violent pathologies of a security state and transform them into a cultural essence.

The Islamic text itself provides the antidote to this clash, not through passive tolerance, but through an epistemological necessity. The Quranic revelation in Surah Al-Hujurat is not a suggestion; it is a structural command:

> "O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another"
>
> Surah Al-Hujurat, 49:13

This is the concept of Ta'aruf. It is a radical epistemology of the Other. It means that the Other is not an enemy to be destroyed, nor a primitive to be civilised, nor a mirror in which to see our own shadow. The Other is a necessary component of our own knowledge. In the Quranic view, God created difference specifically so that we would be forced to engage in the work of "knowing" (*li-ta'arafu*). One cannot know oneself without knowing someone who is not oneself.

The West believes it has all the answers, with its technologies of rights and individualism. But looking at the epidemic of loneliness, the social fragmentation, and the moral confusion of modern Western societies, perhaps it is time to stop preaching and start listening.

We must stop falling for the autocrat's trap. We must stop seeing oppression as "culture." We must recognise that what we call a "clash of values" is actually a clash of ignorance, fuelled by power and sustained by our lazy refusal to look the Other in the eye and see a reflection of ourselves. True progress is not the universalisation of Western "values," but the realisation of Ta'aruf: a world where difference is the engine of wisdom rather than the pretext for war.
